Step 1. Screen: Affinity Analysis to Find Top Candidates

Technical Background

FOPPR enables rapid screening of commercial NfL antibodies by measuring real-time binding kinetics without repeated regeneration steps or extensive optimization to minimize mass transport limitation, analyte retention, and rebinding effects.

For each candidate, FOPPR measures the association rate constant (ka), dissociation rate constant (kd), and equilibrium dissociation constant (KD = kd/ka). These parameters help distinguish antibodies that bind strongly and remain stable from those that produce signal but dissociate quickly.

Experimental Procedure & Data

Nine commercial NfL antibodies were screened to compare their binding affinity for NfL. Their KD values ranged from 1.4 nM to 37.2 nM, where a lower KD indicates stronger overall binding affinity. Two antibodies, A(1) and I(2), showed KD values below 10 nM (1.96 nM and 1.4 nM, respectively), making them primary candidates for epitope binning to determine whether they could work together as a sandwich antibody pair.

Why Real-Time Measurement Matters

Real-time measurement captures the full binding behavior of an interaction: how fast the complex forms, how tightly it binds, and how long it remains associated. Unlike endpoint assays, which provide only a snapshot after incubation and wash steps, real-time methods continuously monitor association and dissociation. This prevents fast-dissociating binders from being misinterpreted as false negatives.

Step 2. Select: Epitope Binning to Identify the Best Antibody Pair

Technical Background

High affinity shows that an antibody binds strongly to the target, but it does not tell us whether two antibodies can bind the target at the same time.

For a sandwich assay, the capture and detection antibodies must recognize distinct, accessible epitopes. If two antibodies bind the same or overlapping epitope, they compete. Even when epitopes are different, one antibody can physically block the other if the sites are too close, a phenomenon known as steric hindrance.

Therefore, affinity alone is not sufficient. Epitope binning is needed to identify antibody pairs that can bind simultaneously and form a functional sandwich assay.

Experimental Procedure & Data

NFL protein is immobilized on the optical fiber. Up to 4 antibodies are introduced sequentially with no regeneration step between injections. The signal after each addition reveals whether the incoming antibody can still access a free epitope on the fiber-bound NfL protein, or whether it is blocked by the preceding antibody.

Blocking — Competing Pair

When G(2) was introduced after I(2) had bound, no significant signal change was observed in the sensorgram, indicating that G(2) and I(2) likely compete for the same or overlapping epitope on NfL.

Non-Blocking — Compatible Pair

When I(1) was introduced after I(2) had bound, the decrease in signal suggested that I(1) was able to bind after I(2), indicating that the two antibodies bind to non-competing sites on NfL.

Unique Feature

FOPPR can sequentially introduce up to four antibodies onto the same target surface to rapidly assess epitope compatibility without regeneration between antibody injections.

A signal decrease suggests that the added antibody recognizes an available, non-competing epitope. A reduced or absent signal change suggests competition, epitope overlap, or steric hindrance.

Step 3. Detect & Compare: Use the Selected Pair for Ultra-Sensitive Detection

Technical Background

With the I(2) + A(1) pair confirmed as both high-affinity and epitope-compatible, the final step is validating detection performance using FONLISA and comparing results against SIMOA

The capture antibody is immobilized on an uncladded glass fiber surface. When NfL is present in the serum sample, it binds the capture antibody and subsequently recruits the detection antibody, which is conjugated to a gold nanoparticle (AuNP). The AuNP sandwich complex enters the evanescent field generated at the fiber surface as light propagates by multiple total internal reflection. The AuNP absorbs light in proportion to the analyte concentration, generating a quantifiable signal drop at the fiber output with near-zero background.

Clinical Validation

FONLISA was tested across two patient cohorts (n = 8 each). Healthy controls and KD patients known to exhibit elevated serum NfL. FONLISA distinguished the groups with statistical significance comparable to SIMOA (p = 0.0002 for controls, p = 0.0009 for KD patients), using less than 20 µL of serum per run.

Why FOPPR Outperforms Conventional SPR for This Task

The dextran matrix problem

Standard SPR sensor chips typically use a thin, flat gold surface modified with a dextran matrix to create a three-dimensional binding environment. Compared with direct immobilization on a flat 2D surface, this matrix increases surface binding capacity and can improve target accessibility, helping reduce orientation-related artifacts. However, matrix-based surfaces can also contribute to analyte retention, rebinding, and mass transport limitations, requiring extensive optimization of assay conditions to obtain reliable kinetic measurements.

FOPPR's 3D desgin for faster kinetic screening

FOPPR uses a self-assembled gold nanoparticle monolayer on a glass fiber to create a three-dimensional sensing surface. This unobstructed architecture allows association and dissociation to occur more freely, avoiding the analyte retention and rebinding effects commonly associated with matrix-based sensor surfaces. FOPPR does not require externally driven flow. Sample delivery is driven by capillary force and gravity, creating a gentler, low-shear environment that better supports native-like binding behavior. As a result, the measured ka and kd values more closely reflect the true antibody–antigen interaction while reducing the need for extensive surface optimization or regeneration, enabling simpler and faster antibody screening.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I replace a discontinued antibody without rebuilding my assay from scratch?
Use FOPPR to screen multiple commercial alternatives simultaneously for binding kinetics (Ka, Kd, KD). From the top-affinity candidates, run epitope binning on a single chip to identify a non-competing pair suitable for a sandwich configuration. Validate the new pair with FONLISA.
How does FOPPR differ from conventional SPR for antibody screening?
SPR dextran matrices increase binding capacity, but may trap analytes and promote rebinding, requiring extensive optimization of ligand density, flow rate, and regeneration conditions. In contrast, FOPPR uses a dextran-free gold nanoparticle 3D surface with capillary- and gravity-driven sample delivery, allowing freer binding and dissociation with minimal optimization. Because FOPPR does not require regeneration between measurements, it enables faster and simpler antibody screening.
How sensitive is FONLISA for NfL detection compared to SIMOA?
An R value of 0.98 indicates a strong correlation between FONLISA and SIMOA measurements, meaning both methods show similar NfL trends across patient samples.
How many antibodies can FOPPR screen in a single run?
For epitope binning, FOPPR can sequentially test up to four antibodies on a single chip without regeneration between injections. For ultrasensitive detection, each chip measures one antibody–analyte interaction at a time. However, the D8 system can run up to eight chips in parallel, enabling efficient screening of multiple candidates.
